General Responsibilities

I graduated from the School of Zoology in 2000. I have since completed postdoctoral positions at Texas A&M University, Deakin University, University of Otago, and CSIRO Marine & Atmospheric Research. I have worked on a variety of taxa, mostly fishes, but also freshwater and terrestrial invertebrates and vertebrates (crayfish, owls, penguins, native ).

Research Interests

My research interests encompass three disciplines. Firstly, I am interested in the reconstruction of phylogenetic relationships based on DNA sequences to address questions of biogeography and earth history, trait evolution, macroecology, and taxonomy. Secondly, I am interested in the use of molecular data (predominantly microsatellites) for the inference of historical and contemporary movement of individuals among populations, and changes in population size (impact of perturbations, past climate changes etc.). I am particularly interested in the incorporation of earth science and spatial information science (i.e. GIS) into the above disciplines. I am also interested in the study of evolution at the molecular level, particularly that involving the molecular clock hypothesis, but also mitochondrial genomics.

The School of Zoology is a founding member of the Molecular Genetics Laboratory, which houses the necessary infrastructure for molecular genetic research.

My research is focussed on four topics at present:

  • Biogeography: assessing the past roles of dispersal, vicariance, and extinction on contemporary distributions (e.g. Gondwanan vicariance, antitropicality).
  • Impact of landscape evolution and historical climate change on the distribution of taxa (e.g. the impacts of river piracy on freshwater-limited taxa).
  • Gene flow among populations and the influence of intervening environments (e.g. oceanographic breaks)
  • Factors influencing rates of molecular evolution (e.g. metabolic rate, time dependency).

Honours and postgraduate projects are available in each of these themes.

Research Project/s

  • Evolution and dispersal in glaciated landscapes: the phylogeography of the spotted snow skink (Niveoscincus ocellatus) in Tasmania.
  • Genetic connectivity of local kelp species in relation to marine park design
  • Impacts of demographic, anthropogenic, and epidemic effects on phylogeography of the Tasmanian devil (Sarcophilus harrisii) inferred from mtDNA, microsatellites and SNP data
  • The effects of forest influence and successional stage on beetle diversity
