Research Interests

 

 

I am a postdoc on ARC Linkage Project (LP110200805) that I helped to coordinate the application for during my PhD. My position is 90% research and 10% teaching. I have considerable postgraduate supervision and mentoring responsibilities (currently co-supervise 2 PhD and mentoring 3 PhD students).

I have two research foci:
(i) Adaptive population genomics. Using next-generation sequencing approaches to investigate species demographic history, species boundaries of species complexes, and explore genomic targets of environmental selection. 
(ii) Seed sourcing for restoration. Together with PhD students I co-supervise (Nicholas Gellie, Kimberly McCallum), working on (a) empirically testing alternative provenancing strategies for ecological restoration, and (b) quantifying the biodiversity and ecosystem benefits of different restoration approaches (e.g. monoculture vs. diverse plantings).

Significant awards:
• Early Career Research Award (within 5 years of PhD), Faculty of Sciences, The University of Adelaide
• Executive Dean's Order of Merit, Faculty of Sciences, The University of Adelaide

 

I was a postdoc with Prof Jon Ågren’s plant ecology and evolution group. I used quantitative genetic and next-generation sequencing methods, together with extensive field and glass house trials, to explore the genetic basis and adaptive significance of adaptive traits in the model system Arabidopsis thaliana. My position was 95% research and 5% teaching, including ad hoc intensive tutoring on MSc course Evolutionary Processes, and student supervision and mentoring.


Adaptive traits included: 
(i) leaf hairs (a classic plant defence trait). I worked with Prof Douglas Schemske (Michigan State University, USA), Prof John McKay (Colorado State University, USA) and Dr Sverre Lundemo (Uppsala University, Sweden). This resulted in two manuscripts that are in final preparation for submission.
(ii) root microbes (soil-borne microbes in the roots of plants; a metabolic or microbial defence trait). This root microbiome project is ongoing. I worked with Prof Siv Andersson (Uppsala University), Prof Sandra Baldalf (Uppsala University), Assoc Prof Anna Rosling (Uppsala University) and Prof Jan Stenlid (Swedish Agricultural University).

 

Full-time PhD student, The University of Adelaide (supervisors Prof Andrew Lowe, Dr Kym Ottewell, Dr Michael Gardner). 

Significant findings:
• I conclusively showed that pollen diversity could have fitness effects over and above those of inbreeding, establishing a new paradigm for studying gene flow and mating systems of plants. I achieved this by the integration of population genetics with common garden trials, using a similar design to that proposed here. I showed this effect in a continent-wide survey of seed provenances of big-leaf mahogany (Swietenia macrophylla); the world’s most highly prized timber species and an ecosystem keystone species (Breed MF et al. 2012. Shifts in reproductive assurance strategies and inbreeding costs associated with habitat fragmentation in Central American mahogany. Ecology Letters 15(5): 444-452). 
• I proposed the ‘constrained inbreeding hypothesis’ to potentially explain these pollen diversity effects (Breed MF et al. 2014 PLoS ONE). 
• I successfully applied for grants to cover all financial requirements of my PhD (consumables: AU$100k; travel: AU$12k).
• I was included in the coordination process of a successful ARC Linkage project (LP110200805) with Profs Andrew Lowe, Corey Bradshaw and Barry Brook. My role included helping generate novel approaches and negotiate support from industry partners ($120K cash and $1.4M in-kind).
