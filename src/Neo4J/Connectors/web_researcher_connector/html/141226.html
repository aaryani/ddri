Research Details

 

I am an evolutionary biologist with a particular interest in sexual selection and the evolution of reproductive traits. This means that my research focuses on a range of research questions including: male-male competition, sperm competition, female mate choice, trade-offs and co-evolutionary dynamics. My research is question-driven and aims to test predictions from theory. Therefore, I’m fairly taxonomically promiscuous and work on species that can best address the question I’m interested in. In the past I’ve worked with fish (salmon, cichlids, sharks, guppies, rainbowfish), marine mammals, social insects, and marine invertebrates in an effort to better understand the evolution of reproductive traits.

My research is both field and lab based and varies depending on the research question being asked. But generally I prefer to mix these two approaches in order to design tightly controlled experiments while not losing sight of what happens in the real world. I apply a range of approaches to my research including phylogenetic comparative methods, multivariate selection analyses, complex statistical modeling, and meta-analyses. Most of these techniques have scary names but can be mastered with a little work and I’m happy to teach you how to use them if you are interested in working in my group.

 

My main research interests include:

 

Selection on the ejaculate: Sperm experience intense selective pressures. Natural selection acts to ensure male fertility while sexual selection acts to make a male’s ejaculate more competitive than rival males. My research aims to quantify how these forms of selection are acting on the ejaculate while taking into account the complex interactions between sperm form and function. This reserach primarily focuses on marine invertebrates, but my aim is to also examine vertebrate model systems in the near future. 

 

Sperm and genital evolution: Sperm competition is a major selective force influence the evolution of reproductive traits. Using phylogenetic comparative approaches I am interested in understanding how sperm competition influences sperm quality and genital morphology. This reserach examines evolutionary correlations among reproductive traits and assesses how fast these traits evolve over evolutionary time. This research focuses on African cichlids from Lake Tanganyika, fishes generally, marine mammals (pinnipeds and cetaceans), social insects and sharks.

 

Sperm competition and alternative reproductive tactics: Not all males use the same behaviours to reproduce. In some species, larger males court and guard females to secure fertilizations, while other smaller sneaker males perform sneaky fertilizations by releasing their sperm at the same time as the larger male. In these species, sneaker males experience a greater level of sperm competition because they always release sperm in the presence of another male. My research has examines the reproductive consequences of these different mating behaviours of males within the same species. This research focuses on African cichlids, freshwater gobies and intertidal marine fish.

 

Reproductive consequences of inbreeding: Inbreeding can have profound fitness costs, yet we know little about its impact on reproductive traits. This is surprising given that these traits are thought to be particularly vulnerable to genetic stress caused by inbreeding. Using guppies as a model system I am examining the reproductive costs of inbreeding and assessing how females avoid these costs.
