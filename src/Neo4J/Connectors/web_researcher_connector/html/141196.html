Angela Eads

Thesis

Effects of environmental stress on early life-history traits in marine broadcast spawners

Summary

This project aims to explore the effects of ocean warming and acidification on the gamete traits and reproductive success of two local marine broadcast spawners, the blue mussel Mytilus galloprovincialis and the purple sea urchin Heliocidaris erythrogramma armigera. Specifically, this project aims to understand the tolerance of gametes and early embryos to projected changes in ocean temperature and acidity, and the potential for these taxa to respond adaptively to these unprecedented challenges.

Why my research is important

The unique biodiversity of Australian marine life currently faces significant and unprecedented threats due to anthropogenic climate change and concomitant changes to ocean chemistry, namely ocean warming and acidification. It is vital that we understand how Australian marine life will be affected by predicted environmental changes, and how species may be able to respond. In particular, there is a pressing need to determine whether populations can tolerate a new range of ocean conditions, and to determine their evolutionary potential to adapt these changes.

Marine invertebrates play a major role in ocean communities, often providing habitat, food and refuge for numerous other species. Many marine invertebrates release their gametes directly into the water (known as broadcast spawning) where fertilization, embryonic and larval development then take place. Consequently, any disruption to fertilization and subsequent developmental stages will directly influence population size, structure, and persistence. Therefore, understanding the sensitivity and adaptive capacity of these early life-history stages to changing ocean conditions is paramount when investigating the biological impacts of ocean warming and acidification.

This information will be of key interest to both evolutionary biologists (from a fundamental perspective) and policy makers (from a more applied perspective).
