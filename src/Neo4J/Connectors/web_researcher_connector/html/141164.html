Dr Christofer Clemente

  • Overview

Overview

I am interested in the relationship between form, function and ecology of living and extinct animals. At the University of Western Australia I studied vision in spiders, morphology, metabolic rates and biomechanics in lizards and compared these to ecological characteristics. I have similarly studied the evolution of bipedalism in dragon lizards, showing lizards were essentially popping a wheelie. Latter my research at the University of Cambridge focused on insect adhesion, examining the multitude of solutions insects have developed to overcome the problems of sticking to smooth surfaces. At Harvard University I examined the vertebrate muscle system, specifically how muscle mechanics integrate with the environment dynamically, during locomotion. My current research at the University of Queensland continues my research into lizard locomotion, with a focus on the design of biologically inspired climbing robots.

Research Interests

  • Comparative biomechaics
    Study of how animals move

Research Impacts

This research has broad impacts and benefits to health, the environment or quality of life beyond academia. By using lizards as model systems, we can develop robots which are capable of performing search and resuce missions, beyond the capabilities of that which is currently available. Further studies into the ecology of species gives us a better understanding of best to implement conservation polocy.
